### The Function of carbohydrate in the Body's Metabolism and Energy Production

Carbohydrates serve as the body's main energy informant. They are metabolized into glucose, which is then utilized by the body's cells for energy. Carbohydrate-restrictive diet, such as the ketogenic or low-carb, high-fat (LCHF) diets, have seen a surge in popularity imputable to their potential weighting loss benefits and their role in managing certain health conditions. The Institute of medicament suggests a recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for carbohydrates for adults of 130 grams per day, which is the minimum carbohydrate requirement for proper brain function (Institute of Medicine, 2005).

### Potential Immediate and Long-Term Consequences of Carbohydrate Elimination

#### Immediate Effects

In the short term, carbohydrate elimination can result in rapid weight loss, mainly due to a decrease in water weight and a reduction in glycogen stores (Gibson et al., 2015). However, this can also lead to fatigue, as the body adapts to using fat and ketones as its primary energy source instead of glucose. Additionally, individuals may experience humour fluctuations, including irritability and difficulty concentrating, as the brain adjusts to the reduced glucose availability (Brinkworth et al., 2009).

Carbohydrates play a role in the production of neurotransmitters, such as serotonin, which regulates mood. Glucose is particularly vital for the brain, which cannot readily use other energy sources. Moreover, the high intake of saturated fats, often associated with low-carb diets, can elevate cholesterol levels, increasing the risk of cardiovascular disease (Siri-Tarino et al., 2010).
